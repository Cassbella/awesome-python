That sounds like a powerful concept—but it’s important to draw a clear line between legitimate OSINT and tools that could cross into surveillance or privacy violations.

A system that passively maps public radio signals (like Wi-Fi SSIDs, Bluetooth beacons, or cell tower IDs) can fall under open-source intelligence (OSINT) when it’s used responsibly—similar to tools like WiGLE or basic spectrum analyzers. Those are often used for things like network troubleshooting, academic research, or understanding signal environments.

Where your “WireTapper” idea becomes risky is in how it’s applied:
	•	Tracking people or personal devices (phones, headphones, cars) can quickly violate privacy laws in many countries
	•	Identifying CCTV systems or IoT devices in a way that exposes vulnerabilities could be misused
	•	Aggregating and mapping data in real time might cross into surveillance, even if technically “passive”
	•	Some jurisdictions treat certain forms of signal interception as illegal—even without decrypting content

There’s a big difference between:
	•	✔️ Mapping anonymous signal presence (e.g., “there are 12 Wi-Fi networks here”)
	•	❌ Profiling or tracking individuals/devices over time

If you’re thinking about building something like this, a safer and more defensible direction would be:
	•	Focus on aggregate, anonymized data
	•	Make it opt-in or transparency-first
	•	Avoid anything that can identify or track specific users/devices
	•	Position it for legitimate use cases like:
	•	RF environment analysis
	•	Smart city planning (with consent)
	•	Network optimization
	•	Security auditing (authorized only)
